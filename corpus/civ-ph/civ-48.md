# civ-48 - civ-ph Placement

## Where This Sits

This chapter closes the French Revolution trilogy by showing Napoleon as the figure who turns revolutionary disruption into imperial myth. The lecture links Robespierre, talent promotion, military charisma, nationalism, and empire into one post-revolutionary settlement.

## Reading Posture

Read it as a strong transition from revolutionary purity to imperial organization. The chapter is not simply about Napoleon as a great man; it is about the conditions that let a new political-military figure gather the Revolution into a durable myth.

## Historical Pressure Points

- The Revolution breaks older hierarchy and opens a path for new talent.
- Robespierre is treated as a necessary precursor to Napoleon.
- Napoleon concentrates military imagination, meritocratic promotion, and imperial image-making.
- Modern political analogy is used to show how myth can outlive institutional detail.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The placement is strong, but the Robespierre-to-Napoleon causal chain, talent-promotion model, great-leader framing, nationalism claims, and modern political analogies must remain bounded pending external review.

## Agency And Necessity

- **Visible actor:** Napoleon.
- **Underlying pressure:** Revolutionary mobilization, mass army, talent promotion, nationalism, and imperial myth make Napoleon legible as more than an individual commander.
- **Tolstoy question:** Does Napoleon master the Revolution, or does the Revolution become legible through Napoleon?
- **Limit:** Do not reduce the French Revolution sequence to biography or charisma.
