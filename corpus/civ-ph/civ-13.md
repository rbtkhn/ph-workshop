# civ-13 - civ-ph Placement

## Where This Sits

This chapter explains how Greek civilization becomes teachable, portable, and institutional. Aristotle sits after Alexander because the lecture treats knowledge as part of Hellenistic statecraft, not simply private philosophy.

## Reading Posture

Read it as an infrastructure chapter. The point is not only what Aristotle thought, but how Greek categories could be gathered, sorted, taught, and transmitted across a wider imperial world.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Aristotle is contrasted with Plato through purpose, motion, and the prime mover.
- Knowledge organization is linked to Macedonian and Hellenistic power.
- Alexandria and the Museum stand for institutionalized learning.
- Greek education becomes a later bridge into Jewish and Christian worlds.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The frame is strong because Aristotle is a major civilizational transmitter, but the state knowledge project claim needs careful later checking against manuscript history, Aristotle's school, and Hellenistic institutions.
