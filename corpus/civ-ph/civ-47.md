# civ-47 - civ-ph Placement

## Where This Sits

This chapter places Robespierre at the religious-political center of the French Revolution sequence. After Rousseau supplies the dream of a rational promised land, Robespierre appears as the figure who tries to make that promise real through virtue, fear, sacrifice, and revolutionary purity.

## Reading Posture

Read it as a strong but high-risk hinge. The chapter is most useful when its religious analogies are treated as interpretive tools for revolutionary energy, not as proof that Robespierre literally fits the theological roles named in the lecture.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Rousseau provides the revolutionary dream that civ-47 puts under pressure.
- Robespierre is treated as prophet, purifier, and sacrificial figure.
- The Terror is read through virtue, fear, pollution, and revolutionary survival.
- The chapter prepares Napoleon by showing what revolutionary myth and sacrifice leave behind.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The placement is strong, but claims about Robespierre, Jesus analogy, human sacrifice, scapegoating, and revolutionary violence must remain bounded as lecture interpretations pending external historical review. Guardrails belong in this orientation and commentary, not in the verbatim transcript.
