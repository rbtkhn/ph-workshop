# Civilization Museum Exhibit: Civilization #51: Shakespeare's Language of Empire

## How To Use This Exhibit

Use this exhibit before reading the transcript or commentary. It gives students a curated artifact doorway into the lecture: objects, texts, maps, places, diagrams, music, speeches, documents, performances, and pressure systems, while keeping source claims, interpretation, and rights status separate. This is a curated draft awaiting human search and curation.

## Entry Object

### 01. First Folio title page

- **Type:** manuscript
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Shakespeare_First_Folio_Title_Page.jpg
- **Rights:** external_link_only
- **What to notice:** The First Folio makes Shakespeare visible as a canon-forming artifact, not just a playwright.
- **Limit:** The Folio is a posthumous publication and reflects editorial mediation.

## Context Anchors

### 02. Globe Theatre reconstruction

- **Type:** place
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Shakespeare%27s_Globe
- **Rights:** external_link_only
- **What to notice:** The theater locates Shakespeare in a commercial, urban, public language machine.
- **Limit:** Modern reconstructions are teaching aids, not direct Elizabethan evidence.

### 03. Elizabeth I Armada portrait

- **Type:** portrait
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Queen_Elizabeth_I_%28%27The_Armada_Portrait%27%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg
- **Rights:** external_link_only
- **What to notice:** The portrait places Shakespeare near imperial self-fashioning, maritime power, and national myth.
- **Limit:** Court portraiture is propaganda-rich and should be read as constructed image.

## Primary Objects And Texts

### 04. Map of early modern London

- **Type:** map
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Maps_of_London_in_the_16th_century
- **Rights:** external_link_only
- **What to notice:** London's density helps explain the theatrical public that could turn language into empire-scale identity.
- **Limit:** Map categories contain varied dates and purposes; item-level dating matters.

## Comparison Objects

### 05. Othello title page

- **Type:** text
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Othello
- **Rights:** external_link_only
- **What to notice:** A single play object opens questions of race, empire, military service, intimacy, and English self-definition.
