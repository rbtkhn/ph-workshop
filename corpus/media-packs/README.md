# Predictive History Museum

The Predictive History Museum is the public artifact-exhibit layer for Predictive History. The repo path remains `corpus/media-packs/` for compatibility, but the reader-facing surface is the museum: one curated artifact exhibit per chapter.

The canonical storage and bidder contract lives in [docs/predictive-history-museum.md](../../docs/predictive-history-museum.md).

The museum covers both halves of the project:

- **Predictive History: Civilization:** one exhibit for each Civilization-facing chapter.
- **Predictive History: Apocalypse:** one exhibit for each Apocalypse-facing chapter.

Each exhibit helps students enter a chapter before reading the transcript or commentary.

Artifacts can be visual, textual, sonic, spatial, institutional, or symbolic: objects, texts, maps, places, diagrams, music, speeches, documents, performances, architecture, flags, tools, propaganda, and pressure systems.

The human role is curatorial judgment: selection, taste, proportion, cultural context, emotional calibration, and final responsibility. Agents structure selected artifacts, render museum-label captions, and validate links, return paths, and status fields. Rights handling remains conservative and procedural: link first, avoid mirrored binaries, and mark uncertain items for review.

Predictive History Museum exhibits orient; they do not prove lecture claims or replace source transcripts, commentary, civ-ph placement cards, or correction paths.

## Exhibit Shape

- **Entrance Artifact:** the one artifact that opens the chapter's world.
- **Context Room:** maps, places, institutions, timelines, or background artifacts that locate the chapter.
- **Primary Artifacts And Texts:** objects, documents, texts, recordings, or artifacts closest to the lecture's subject.
- **Comparison Artifacts:** artifacts that help readers see contrast, reception, memory, or alternative framing.
- **Pressure Systems:** diagrams, infrastructure, weapons, finance, geography, institutions, or other pressure fields.
- **Caution Room:** artifacts that are powerful but easy to overread.

Each wall label should include what to notice and what this cannot prove.

## Human Curator Checklist

- **Selection taste:** choose the few artifacts that open the chapter, not everything available.
- **Cultural judgment:** notice sacred, traumatic, political, identity-bearing, or contested material.
- **Historical proportion:** keep one striking artifact from overpowering the lecture's argument.
- **Aesthetic pacing:** build entrance, contrast, surprise, quiet, and return.
- **Rights prudence:** prefer stable external links and mark uncertain reuse status for review.
- **Representative balance:** avoid only empire, elites, victors, state propaganda, or famous men.
- **Emotional calibration:** decide how close to bring readers to violence, grief, awe, humiliation, or beauty.
- **Pedagogical fit:** include artifacts because they help readers enter the chapter, not because they decorate it.
- **Local knowledge:** add non-obvious songs, documents, places, tools, rituals, or performances an agent might miss.
- **Final responsibility:** own the exhibit's point of view and limits.
