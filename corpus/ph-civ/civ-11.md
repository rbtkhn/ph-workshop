# civ-11 - PH-CIV Placement

## Where This Sits

This chapter is the hinge between Greek civic-intellectual formation and the imperial spread of Greek culture. Philip II matters because he turns Macedon from an exposed northern kingdom into the machine that will carry Greek forms beyond the polis.

## Reading Posture

Read it as a state-formation chapter before reading it as Alexander background. The lecture's strongest move is to shift attention from the famous conqueror to the quieter builder of military discipline, elite loyalty, money, diplomacy, and timing.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Greek culture is framed as spreading through conquest, not only diffusion.
- Philip becomes the founder figure behind Alexander's later expansion.
- Macedon's poverty and vulnerability are treated as pressures that sharpen cohesion.
- Chaeronea marks the defeat of the old Greek city-state balance.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The frame is strong because the chapter explains the institutional bridge from Greece to Hellenistic empire, but the Korea comparison and assassination theories should remain bounded teaching devices rather than settled evidence.
