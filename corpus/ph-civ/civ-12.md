# civ-12 - PH-CIV Placement

## Where This Sits

This chapter carries the Macedonian hinge into empire. Alexander is not presented merely as a great conqueror but as the figure through whom Greek culture becomes Hellenistic world culture at the cost of escalating personal rule.

## Reading Posture

Read with two scales at once: the operational scale of armies, cities, and battles, and the inner scale of glory, insecurity, and tyranny. The chapter asks what happens when conquest becomes both world-making and self-consuming.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Philip's state becomes Alexander's conquest platform.
- Thebes and Persia show fear, speed, cohesion, and battlefield risk.
- Divine kingship and Persian customs shift conquest into ruler mythology.
- India and the desert march mark the practical and moral limits of expansion.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The frame is strong because Alexander changes the scale of Greek civilization, but the moral diagnosis of tyranny should be held beside later checks on logistics, Macedonian court politics, Persian evidence, and Hellenistic institutions.
