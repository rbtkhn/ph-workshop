# civ-56 - PH-CIV Placement

## Where This Sits
This chapter carries the German idealist bridge into Marx. It places dialectical materialism, class struggle, capitalism, communism, alienation, religion, status, China, Russia, and Korea inside the course's larger question of what really drives history.

## Reading Posture
Read it as a strong modernity hinge, not as final political economy. The lecture is most useful when Marx is treated as both a powerful critic of capitalism and a failed prophet whose model is being tested against religion, status, psychology, and twentieth-century communist states.

## Historical Pressure Points
- Marx inverts Hegel into dialectical materialism and class struggle.
- Capitalism is treated as imperial, consolidating, and alienating.
- Religion, God, and status are used to challenge economic determinism.
- China, Russia, North Korea, South Korea, capitalism, and communism make the chapter politically high-risk.

## Limits of the Frame
This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The placement is strong, but Marx/Piketty/Quigley summaries, capitalism-vs-communism generalizations, North/South Korea comparisons, China education claims, and sweeping statements about religion, status, and human motivation must remain bounded pending external review.

## Return Path
Return here when later chapters discuss Freud, modernism, nation-state ideology, Soviet communism, capitalism, alienation, political religion, China, Korea, class struggle, or the limits of economic explanations of history.
