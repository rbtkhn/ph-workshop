# The Tolstoy Question

This corridor asks who, or what, makes history. It begins from Tolstoy's challenge to great-man history: the named actor may be visible, but visibility is not the same thing as causation.

Use this path as a study route through visible actors, deeper pressures, myth, institution, and necessity. It is not a deterministic proof, and it does not claim that any chapter resolves the problem. It gives readers a repeatable question to carry across the corpus:

> When a great figure appears to make history, what deeper pressure made that figure possible?

## How To Read It

Read each node twice. First ask what the named actor does. Then ask what had to exist before that actor could matter: institutions, myths, technologies, publics, enemies, incentives, and constraints.

The corridor should sharpen rather than flatten agency. A strong reading can say both that Napoleon matters and that Napoleon is unintelligible without revolutionary mobilization, mass armies, nationalism, bureaucracy, and imperial myth.

## Guardrail

This route asks a causation question; it does not install a single causation doctrine. Do not use it to erase moral responsibility, excuse violence, or reduce history to impersonal necessity. The point is to prevent retrospective theater from being mistaken for explanation.
