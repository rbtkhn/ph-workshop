# Game Theory #16: Pax Judaica Rising

## Part I: Full transcript

Today we will talk about how this war will end. It is impossible to predict exactly when the war will end, but I would like to make a few predictions about how it will end.

First, let's look at some video clips to understand the current war situation.

Here is President Donald Trump. We're talking about war. In the clip he describes how the fighting spread across Qatar, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, and Kuwait — no one expected it and many were shocked. Other countries fought back. Iran could have surrendered, but think about it: if we weren't there, they would have enormous power. Iran had thousands of missiles, most of which the US and allies destroyed. There were also thousands of drones; they were producing drones and missiles all over the place, but most of those factories were destroyed.

What he's saying, in effect, is that the plan was to attack Iran, remove its leadership, and force surrender — but the enemy counterattacked in ways that were not expected. He never thought Iran would counterattack and block his return flight; he never thought they would strike Qatar, Bahrain, and Kuwait in the way that unfolded. So the United States planned and attacked, but the other side answered in ways the planners did not foresee.

Next, White House messaging. A spokesperson says, in essence: there is no need to destroy certain points; Iran failed to accept the reality of the current situation and was defeated militarily; if you don't understand that, you will continue to lose; President Trump will deliver an unprecedented and powerful blow; he is not bluffing; he is ready to bring hell if necessary.

The message is: we defeated them, but for some reason they don't know it. The problem is not us — we went in with the right strategy. Iran is so stubborn or blind that it doesn't even know it has lost.

Ask yourself: in modern history, has a modern army — with navy, air force, air defense, leadership, and deep bunkers like Iran's — ever simply vanished in an instant? Historically, it has not been destroyed by overwhelming firepower alone in such a short time.

The airstrikes the United States and Israel carried out were truly historic in scale. The argument from the administration is that the President freed the hands of forces going to war so they could close with the enemy and destroy with maximum brutality from the beginning.

That is also why they say they are "negotiating" — negotiating with bombs. As they wait above Tehran, the President speaks about the other side's future and offers a choice. The line is that the United States will not allow Iran to have nuclear weapons; the Department of Defense agrees; the job is to ensure that — and that nuclear options will be used as strongly as "necessary" to advance American interests.

Here is the Secretary of Defense speaking for the Pentagon. His attitude is that we are winning by a landslide and there is no need to negotiate; we will keep winning by a landslide. The military posture is: you will get the job done.

Yet American media report that President Trump is seeking negotiations. The military message and the political message do not match — one side says negotiation, the other says we are negotiating with ordnance.

On the economic side, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent discusses Russian oil and additional supplies. The administration expected bottlenecks — temporary ones, he stresses. Something on the order of 130 million barrels of crude may sit at sea in the coming days; sanctions on Iranian crude at sea might be lifted — roughly 140 million barrels depending on how you count. That is on the order of ten days to two weeks of what Iran had been exporting — oil that would largely have gone to China. The plan, as he presents it, is to use Iranian crude to keep prices down for that window.

Bessent also says the United States would block Iran's oil exports if necessary — that they had answers for unexpected problems. If the disruption lasts too long, the global economy could be thrown into chaos.

There is a darkly ironic reading he invites: we knew this could happen and had an ingenious plan — let Iran make money selling oil, then hit them. In practice, the United States lifted sanctions on Iranian oil; Iran could earn on the order of fourteen billion dollars quickly, while its annual military budget is roughly ten billion. The administration's story is: we knew Iran might try to break the global economy, so we made Iran rich so it could no longer destroy the world economy — "now we've got them."

Iran remains dangerous. If Hormuz shipping is threatened, oil could sit above a hundred dollars for years; it might approach a hundred fifty. That would mean global recession. Some officials tell the BBC the war should not stop now because even if it stops, Iran would still control the oil. What that line leaves out is that if the war had not started, there would have been no excuse to close the Strait of Hormuz in the first place. The reply is: don't worry — the war has started, so we must fight to the end.

Now look at a drone launched by Iran's proxies in Iraq, flying over a US military base. It is hard to stop. It is remotely controlled, passes over the base, spots helicopters, and destroys one. US bases across the Middle East are vulnerable to cheap drones — there is no airtight protection.

From the American perspective, then, the war either bankrupts the United States or leads to defeat. Fighting a determined enemy is very difficult.

Okay — how does this war unfold in theory?

As I have said many times, America is losing this war. The main reason is arrogance. Let's look at what that means strategically and on the battlefield.

War takes place on four levels: the narrative level — how world public opinion and historians will judge it; the political dimension — how allies and domestic supporters see it; the economic dimension; and the military dimension.

If you look closely, Iran is waging war in a very different way than the United States. The United States is trying to bend narrative, politics, and economics to fit its military strategy. The general US approach is to obtain surrender by eliminating leadership or wrecking the economy, then impose terms. Washington expects NATO allies, media, and markets to support that military design.

Politically, for example, Trump ordered NATO to help open a corridor; he also refuses to acknowledge how unpopular the war is at home. Economically, the administration tries to keep oil prices low. He tells reporters the war is going well and threatens that if they don't report it that way, they could face jail. That is coercion across narrative, politics, and economics to align with military strategy.

Iran does the opposite. It uses military power to influence the economy, politics, and public opinion. Military strategy is tailored to help the Iranian economy. Iran strategically controls traffic for Chinese ships — the main buyers of Iranian oil. Ships from Japan, Qatar, and Oman may pass if they show a cooperative attitude.

Politically, Iran tries to divide the Gulf: Saudi Arabia and the UAE favor regime change in Iran; Qatar and Oman are on another side.

Iran also works on world opinion so that history records it as being on the correct side. Many people believe it — including many Americans who think Iran can win.

These are two different strategies.

Consider the strengths and weaknesses.

From the US side, forcing the world to align with your military strategy means little introspection. When you sit with a strategist and ask how the war is going, it is hard to discuss real course corrections.

Iran can reflect because its military effort has to stay aligned with economics, politics, and narrative.

Second: flexibility. The United States lacks the habit of changing course when things go wrong. It doubles down — more bombing — even when that may yield nothing, and may escalate toward desperate moves such as ground troops.

Iran can adjust military strategy to fit economic and political reality — for example, imposing tolls on ships in the Strait of Hormuz.

Third: resilience. If everyone else must adapt to your military strategy, they will not stay with you when you lose troops and equipment. Resupply becomes painful.

Iran has the opposite dynamic: as it gains narrative support, economic breathing room, and political allies, it can attract foreign fighters, support, and sympathy.

For these reasons, I think America will lose the war. You never know exactly when, because America is an empire and can prolong a war for decades — twenty years or more. But ultimately, I believe America loses.

Now that we have discussed why the United States is likely to lose, let's look at how the world may change and what the end of the war might look like.

We need our model of how the world works. Remember when we discussed world structure?

At the core sits an empire. The empire supplies the military force that draws the outer outline. Inside the empire you have finance — London, Wall Street, the Bank for International Settlements, asset managers — the players who really set the rules of the game.

Empires are about power; finance sets the rules. They build a global economy on the US dollar. But you cannot tell people plainly that this is the structure, or they won't want to play. So multilateral bodies — the United Nations, the WTO, and so on — are built in the name of a "rules-based international order." That language hides the real relationship between finance and empire.

Culture, education, and media justify the system.

Above the state sit organizations that maintain it: intelligence agencies, criminal networks, scientific institutions. Behind them lie transnational capital, secret societies, elite families — sometimes bound by occult ideas.

Understand that many classes of people benefit from the system and want to keep it.

At the center of this machine is an empire that may lose a war with Iran. If the American empire falls, the whole scaffolding may fall. So the systemic fix is not to abolish empire but to **replace** American muscle with another empire that can power the same structure.

I have said this before: in an important sense this is a war involving the United States **and** Israel. Because the American empire has grown corrupt, incompetent, and arrogant, Israel sees a chance to replace American primacy in the Middle East. Eventually, members of the system may agree.

So the main outcome I foresee when this war ends is that **Israel replaces the United States** as the regional imperial power in the Middle East.

Why is the American empire failing? The biggest single factor is the military-industrial complex.

For most of US history there was no large standing army; the Founders saw a standing army as a threat to freedom. World Wars I and II forced the creation of one. After 1945, the complex wanted unlimited funding — hence the Cold War, then the war on terror: endless war to justify the budget.

Julian Assange put it starkly: the point is not necessarily to win the war; the point is to move taxpayer money to the national elite through endless war.

The United States accounts for about forty-one percent of global military spending; China about eight percent; Russia about four. Yet the US military may be among the most corrupt institutions on Earth.

On September 10, 2001, Donald Rumsfeld announced that trillions of dollars could not be accounted for at the Pentagon.

Money flows to contractors. Boeing, for example, takes vast government sums while spending heavily on lobbying. Whistleblowers who tried to expose corruption at Boeing have died in suspicious circumstances before testifying.

That corruption creates three problems for the United States in a war like this:

1. **Lack of political will** — the war is unpopular at home (on the order of forty percent support). Americans will not easily accept heavy costs or a draft.

2. **Lack of production capacity** — the US cannot quickly replace planes, bombs, and equipment at scale.

3. **Casualty aversion** — the Pentagon hides or minimizes losses to avoid political backlash.

Those problems are not easy to fix. So America will probably lose — which gives Israel an opening.

Israel can try to prove it should be the new muscle: **resolution** (willingness to do whatever it takes), **competence** (production and strategy), **determination** (willingness to take casualties).

It has shown cohesion in Gaza: very high support for harsh measures; large majorities treat the war as existential.

Israel is **auditioning** for empire — showing it can control the Middle East more cheaply and effectively than the US, through targeted operations (the pager attack on Hezbollah) and infiltration (Mossad operations sometimes blurred with "ISIS" branding in the information space).

Historically, empires subcontract violence to mercenaries or "pit bulls." The pit bull often replaces the weakening master — think of patterns in Rome, the Americas, the steppe, and elsewhere.

The Greater Israel project imagines control from the Nile to the Euphrates. Notably, Persia does not fit that biblical map. One reading is that the real target is to **force the United States out** of the Middle East so Israel can dominate.

After the war, two strong regional powers may remain: **Israel** and **Iran**. They may respect each other and divide influence by a harsh logic: the strong respect each other and take advantage of the weak.

Israel would control key trade routes, data centers and AI surveillance, and the India–Middle East–Europe corridor (IMEC).

Iran would benefit from lifted sanctions, help control a north–south corridor with Russia, and sit on Belt and Road routes.

The GCC states are weak and divided; they will lean toward whichever strong power offers protection.

That is what I call **Pax Judaica** — a new order in which Israel becomes the dominant power in the Middle East, replacing a declining American empire.

This class is about intelligent guessing and sharpening critical thinking. It is not prophecy. If events go another way, we analyze what we got wrong.

Let me be clear about language. I am **not** saying Israel will defeat the United States in a head-to-head war. I mean that Israel is exploring the possibility of replacing the United States as the **imperial power in the Middle East** when America withdraws or collapses there. Israel is trying to show the world's elite that it has the resolution, competence, and determination that America now lacks.
