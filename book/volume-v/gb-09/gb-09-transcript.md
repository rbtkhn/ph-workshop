## Part I: Full transcript

The Divine Comedy is the greatest literary masterpiece in human history. When Dante wrote it around 1300, he called it La Commedia. The reason is that at this time in history, epic poetry was considered “high” or “tragic” and was usually written in Latin. But Dante believed that epic poetry should be democratic—it should be accessible to the people. That’s why he called it a “comedy” and wrote it in Tuscan, the language of Florence where he lived. Latin was the language of the elite across Europe. Dante wanted ordinary people to be able to read and understand it.

As a result, Dante is a great believer in democracy. Homer, Dante, and Shakespeare were, in their respective languages, the greatest poets because, first and foremost, they believed that poetry should be accessible to the people. They believed in a democratic spirit in poetry, and that made them distinct from the poets of their time.

La Commedia is a response to the Aeneid by Virgil. For the previous thousand years, the Aeneid had been the dominant literary work in Europe and served as the foundation for much of the Catholic Church’s worldview. Remember, when we read the Aeneid, it is fundamentally about duty, piety, and it contrasts sharply with love.

This idea became dominant in the Catholic Church: you are not allowed to access God directly through love. You must obey the Church, because only the Church can guarantee your redemption and salvation. This led to massive corruption within the Catholic Church. It also led to wars throughout Europe because so much power was concentrated in the Church. Many kings fought for control over it, which caused endless conflict and eventually contributed to the splintering of the Church. Dante himself was deeply embroiled in these conflicts.

So he wrote La Commedia so that people could access God directly through love and bypass the corrupted Catholic Church. If you read La Commedia, you’ll find many direct criticisms of the Church.

La Commedia was also written in response to the Aeneid. Like Homer, Dante wants to position love first and foremost. In La Commedia, love is God itself.

These are the basic themes we’ll be exploring throughout this work.

### Structure of The Divine Comedy

There are three distinct parts:

- Inferno (Hell)
- Purgatorio (Purgatory)
- Paradiso (Paradise)

What makes The Divine Comedy so powerful is that Dante uses two major literary devices to construct his universe: structure and paradox.

Imagine La Commedia not just as epic poetry, but as a very complex mathematical puzzle that you must unravel over time.

Inferno is structured as an inverted triangle. Dante and Virgil travel downward through the circles of Hell, almost like descending underground.

Purgatorio is a pyramid or mountain. Dante and Virgil climb upward until they reach Beatrice at the very top.

Paradiso is structured like a solar system, with the Empyrean (where God resides) at the center. Dante travels with Beatrice through the heavenly spheres.

The structure is highly mathematical, rigorous, and symmetrical in a way rarely seen in poetry.

But what makes The Divine Comedy truly distinct and powerful is the use of paradox—or cognitive dissonance.

All great poetry is designed to be memorized and read aloud. It has subtlety and nuance. Think of poetry as a virus: it tries to infiltrate you, subvert you, and remake you. That’s exactly what The Divine Comedy does. On the surface it sounds simple and beautiful, but the more you memorize it and interact with it, the more it enters you and creates cognitive dissonance. It disrupts the normal way you see the world. Your mind subconsciously works to unravel these paradoxes over time.

The more you read The Divine Comedy over decades, the more it reveals itself to you. It is a universe that comes into you and then remakes you. That is its power and beauty.

The trick is to unravel the paradoxes that implant themselves throughout the work.

Another key structural point: in order to reach Paradise, Dante must first enter Inferno. There are different ways to interpret this. The simplest is that to truly discover God, you must experience hell yourself. Good and evil are intertwined. You can never have good without first experiencing evil. Good is not the absence of evil—good is the confrontation and defeat of evil.

Another interpretation is that Inferno represents Virgil and the Aeneid. For Dante to enter heaven, he must first fully recognize the impact of Virgil and the Aeneid on his psyche and his culture, and then overcome it in order to fully embrace the power of God and love. This is the interpretation I want to emphasize as we go along.

### Dante’s Biography

Dante is considered the greatest poet in the Italian language. At this time (around 1300), Italy was divided into competing city-states—Florence, Venice, Genoa, etc.—all vying for power. They were also in conflict with Rome (the Catholic Church), which wanted authority over them, as well as with larger powers like France and the Holy Roman Empire (roughly modern Germany). Within the city-states, rival families constantly fought one another.

Dante lived in a world of constant conflict, violence, and hatred. The central question for him was: How can human beings escape this endless cycle of vengeance, hatred, and war? That is why he wrote The Divine Comedy.

Two major biographical details are essential:

**Political life:** Dante was of noble birth and therefore deeply involved in the conflicts of his time. Northern Italian city-states had two main factions: the Guelphs (who supported the Papacy) and the Ghibellines (who supported the Holy Roman Empire). Dante’s family were Guelphs. Over time the Guelphs won, but then split into Black and White factions, leading to more infighting. Dante eventually grew sick of the constant political hatred and went into exile to focus on his poetry. He never returned to Florence.

**Love for Beatrice:** Dante fell in love with Beatrice at a very young age (he was about nine, she was eight). Her family was of higher aristocratic rank, and marriages at the time were arranged, so both married others. They remained friends, but Dante carried a deep, lifelong love for her in his heart. Beatrice died young (likely in her mid-twenties, possibly in childbirth), but Dante never forgot her. This pure, idealized love for Beatrice becomes the emotional and spiritual foundation of The Divine Comedy.

In the poem, there are many references to the political conflicts, his exile, his criticism of the Church, and his redemptive love for Beatrice.

### Key passages

**Opening lines (Allen Mandelbaum translation):**

When I had journeyed half of our life’s way,
I found myself within a shadowed forest,
for I had lost the path that does not stray.
Ah, it is hard to speak of what it was,
that savage forest, dense and difficult,
which even in recall renews my fear:
so bitter is it that death is hardly more severe.
But to retell the good discovered there,
I’ll also tell the other things I saw.

Dante is in middle age and finds himself lost in a dark forest. He is confused and filled with hatred for the world, which has caused him to lose his connection to God. He is in a deep depression, feeling hopeless. Then Virgil appears—the poet Dante respects most in the world—and promises to guide him out of the forest and toward the light. Dante sees Virgil as a savior, a father, and a teacher.

This is important because, in order for Dante to ultimately overcome the influence of Virgil and the Aeneid, he must first elevate Virgil to a pedestal so that we can clearly see his limitations.

**Later passage (Beatrice speaking from Heaven):**

In Heaven there’s a gentle lady who weeps
for the distress toward which I send you…
…You, Beatrice, true praise of God,
why have you not helped him who loves you so…?

Much of The Divine Comedy is dialogue. Dante wants us to always ask: Why is this speaker saying what they are saying? What is their worldview?

God is the perfect manifestation of love, generosity, and forgiveness. God does not operate on reciprocity. The Catholic Church taught that if you obey, then God will grant you heaven. Dante wants us to understand that this cannot be true. If God is perfect love, He would never demand anything in return. God gives free will. Free will and reciprocity are contradictions.

In this scene, Virgil explains to Dante why Beatrice sent him. He frames it in terms of reciprocity (because Dante loves Beatrice, she helps him). But this contradicts the nature of divine love. Either Virgil is misinterpreting events through his own limited worldview (based on duty and contract from the Aeneid), or Beatrice is speaking to Virgil in terms he can understand. Either way, it shows Virgil’s limitations.

Already at the beginning, we are being asked to question Virgil’s authority. Virgil is an unreliable guide. To truly enter Paradise and discover God, we must recognize this ourselves.

**Crossing into Hell (Charon the ferryman):**

“Woe to you, corrupted souls!
Forget your hope of ever seeing Heaven.
I come to lead you to the other shore,
to the eternal dark, to fire and frost.”
…“And you approaching there, you living soul,
keep well away from these—they are the dead.”

Charon refuses to take Dante because he is still alive. Virgil intervenes, saying their passage has been willed from above. Charon, who himself rejects God and is in Hell, nevertheless obeys. This is a paradox: Charon does not believe in God, yet he obeys what Virgil claims is God’s will. It suggests that Virgil is, in a sense, the master of Hell—because the Aeneid helped create the very emotions (piety, duty, hatred of love, empire) that make Hell possible. The Catholic Church itself was more influenced by the Aeneid than by pure Christian love.

**On the souls entering Hell:**

“My son,” the gracious master said to me,
“those who have died beneath the wrath of God…
…are eager for the river crossing
because celestial justice spurs them on,
so that their fear is turned into desire.”

Key words in Dante: will and desire. Your soul is what you truly want and move toward. These souls are not in Hell simply because they did bad things. They are in Hell because they desire to be there. Their fear has been turned into desire. They choose Hell. Free will is fundamental: you go where your will and desire take you.

**Limbo (where virtuous non-Christians reside):**

Virgil explains that he and other great souls (Homer, Plato, etc.) are in Limbo because they lacked baptism and lived before Christianity. He says they have no hope. Dante is troubled by this.

Later we will see that this is not entirely true. Salvation is ultimately about will and desire, not merely historical accident or formal religion.

**Minos (judge of Hell):**

Minos warns Dante: “Be careful how you enter, whom you trust. The gate is wide, but do not be deceived.” Virgil quickly silences him. This is another clue that we should question Virgil’s reliability.

**The Lustful:**

Virgil names many famous figures who were overcome by lust: Semiramis, Cleopatra, Helen, Achilles, Paris, Tristan… but notably skips naming Dido (the queen from his own Aeneid who killed herself for love).

This is a profound paradox. Virgil created Dido as one of the most realistic and sympathetic characters in the Aeneid, yet he refuses to name her here. Dante, by contrast, elevates his beloved Beatrice to Heaven. The contrast between Virgil condemning Dido to Hell and Dante raising Beatrice to Paradise is striking.

When Dante asks to speak with two souls moving together (Francesca and Paolo), the text explicitly mentions “the ranks where Dido suffers.” Dante names her when Virgil would not—an act of quiet rebellion.

### Conclusion

This is only the beginning of our journey through The Divine Comedy. As you can see, it is extremely complex. You cannot understand it by surface reading alone. You must read with your heart, your imagination, your intuition, and your connection to God. The poem plants paradoxes and cognitive dissonance that work on you over time. It is ultimately a journey into your own heart and faith.
