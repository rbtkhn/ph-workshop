## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture argues that Rome matters because the United States deliberately modeled itself on Rome and because both societies are presented as aggressive war machines. Jiang uses Rome to ask why Rome rose, why it declined, and what its legacy means for the modern world.

The first half focuses on Roman cultural advantages against Greek democracy and other Mediterranean competitors. The lecture contrasts Greek freedom, eudaimonia, and excellence with Roman liberty, res publica, and piety, then argues that Rome could expand because citizenship and identity were flexible enough to absorb outsiders who fought for Rome.

The second half turns to Rome's identity crisis. Jiang treats the empire as a struggle between republican institutions and imperial command, then marks the Edict of Caracalla as a key rupture because universal citizenship allegedly drained Roman identity of value. The chapter ends by using Rome to warn about American violence, immigration, identity, and future conflict.
