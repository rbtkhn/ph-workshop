## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture begins with the eastern Mediterranean world around 1200 BCE: Mycenaean Greece, Anatolia and the Hittites, Canaan, Egypt, Mesopotamia, Iran, Afghanistan, India, the steppe, Cyprus, Iberia, and Britain are presented as an interconnected Bronze Age economy. Bronze is compared to oil because copper and tin must be mined and traded across long distances. Troy is introduced as a strategic logistics and toll hub, while mining, manufacture, trade, and piracy are the major ways to profit from this world.

The chapter then describes the collapse: Mycenaean Greece burns, the Hittite Empire disappears, Canaan changes, and Egypt survives but loses hegemonic power. It presents the Sea Peoples as pirates and refugees moving from the west, then reviews scholarly explanations: invasion, natural disaster, and the current "perfect storm" or systems-collapse model involving earthquakes, cooler climate, and internal revolt. The lecturer accepts that these factors matter but rejects the idea that they explain the deepest cause.

The alternative explanation is elite overproduction. Drawing on Peter Turchin against a simplified Marxian bottom-up revolt model, the lecture argues that collapse often comes from elite infighting rather than popular rebellion alone. Permanent hereditary elites create organization, wealth, and scale, but they also create rent-seeking, debt, debt slavery, and competition among too many elites for too few rent sources. The lecture uses Piketty's capital-growth argument, the Mycenaean palace economy, and resilience metaphors to argue that Bronze Age societies were already structurally unstable before earthquakes, climate, revolt, or invasion knocked them over.

### Core Concepts Defined

- **Bronze Age interconnection**: a world system linked by mining, manufacturing, trade, piracy, and military competition. `civ-06-transcript.md:32-41`
- **Sea Peoples**: the lecture's name for maritime attackers, pirates, and refugees associated with collapse pressure. `civ-06-transcript.md:43-45`
- **Perfect storm / systems collapse**: the consensus model of multiple interacting shocks rather than one cause. `civ-06-transcript.md:46-49`
- **Elite overproduction**: Turchin's theory that too many elites competing for too few positions drives instability. `civ-06-transcript.md:50-54`, `civ-06-transcript.md:68-73`
- **Permanent hereditary elite**: elites whose status, wealth, and privilege are passed to children. `civ-06-transcript.md:54-58`
- **Rent-seeking**: extracting wealth by controlling access to land, credentials, or institutions rather than producing new value. `civ-06-transcript.md:58-64`
- **Debt slavery**: the lecture's outcome when debt and compound interest become impossible to repay. `civ-06-transcript.md:65-68`
- **Palace economy**: Mycenaean central extraction and redistribution through the palace. `civ-06-transcript.md:79-83`
- **Resilience**: a society's ability to survive shocks such as revolt, bad weather, earthquake, or invasion. `civ-06-transcript.md:85-90`
