## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture opens the second semester by linking the first-semester civilizational arc to current conflict and future prediction. It names Ukraine, the Middle East, and the United States against the world as major conflict zones, then argues that historical study should explain present structures and help anticipate future movement.

The main conceptual move is to compare older models of history: cyclical rise-and-fall patterns and linear progress toward truth. Jiang argues that both models are insufficient and proposes the oceanic-currents model, in which empires, borderlands, internal pressures, and social energy move like interacting currents and storms.

The chapter then introduces boundary conditions for collapse and expansion: debt and landlessness, rent-seeking, rat utopia, elite overproduction, and borderland cultures of freedom, egalitarianism, and self-reliance. It closes by previewing Rome, the Holy Roman Empire, and the Vikings as examples that will test the new model.
