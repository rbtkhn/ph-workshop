## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture continues the argument from `civ-01`: if religion helped draw people into settlement and agriculture, then the religious impulse must be older than agriculture itself. The lecture uses Ice Age cave paintings to argue that symbolic and ritual life was already central to humanity tens of thousands of years ago. Cave painting is framed not as decoration or isolated artistic expression, but as ritual performance involving cave space, sound, music, dancing, animal imagery, and shared religious imagination.

The middle of the lecture reconstructs a possible animist worldview. Childbirth, stars, healing, nature, caves, burial, animals, and hunting are organized into a speculative model in which life moves through portals between worlds, all living beings have souls, and ritual restores balance after killing animals for food. The lecture then adds several evidence threads: cave spaces and acoustics, recurring animal-human or shamanic figures, disability and elaborate burial, recurring geometric symbols, psychedelic or visionary experience, Kant's theory that the mind structures reality, and Durkheim's account of religion as the social imagination of collective life.

The lecture closes by contrasting this account with economic and biological models of humanity. It does not deny economic or biological needs, but argues that humans are first religious animals because they need shared meaning, collective imagination, and an answer to why they exist. It also distinguishes later monotheistic hierarchy from an earlier cyclical or river-like religious imagination.
