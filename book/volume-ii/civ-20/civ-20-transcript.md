Okay, so good morning. We finished the Bronze Age today with the Indus Valley Civilization. We've done Egypt, we've done Mesopotamia, and now we're doing the Indus Valley Civilization.

Today I'm going to look at three questions. First of all, what is it that makes the Indus Valley Civilization distinct or different from other civilizations? Second, why did it decline? They reached their peak about 2600 to 1900 BCE, and then for the next three to four hundred years they declined and ultimately disappeared. Why did that happen?

The last question is: what is their legacy? What is their contribution to the development of Western civilization? Or more broadly, to human civilization?

Alright, so let's look at the Indus Valley Civilization. To do so, we need to first understand the world at this time — about 2500 to 2000 BCE. This is what we call the Middle Bronze Age, because most people were using bronze for weapons and tools.

Over here is Egypt. As we discussed, by far Egypt is the wealthiest, most advanced civilization in the Bronze Age. Across the Red Sea is Arabia. Even though Arabia isn't really discussed that much in world history, it's still significant because it's part of the trading network. Arabia had many nomads who took goods from Mesopotamia over to Egypt and then into the Mediterranean, which accessed all of Europe.

This is Sumer, the beginning of the Mesopotamian civilization. Up here is Anatolia and the Levant. And then the Indus Valley Civilization is over here. Today it would cover the countries of Afghanistan, Pakistan, and northwest India.

What makes the Indus Valley Civilization important is that it's really by the coast, which allows it to access Mesopotamia and Egypt. Meaning, during the Bronze Age, these three civilizations — Egyptians, Mesopotamians, and the Indus Valley Civilization — were heavily interconnected through trade.

What was unique to the Indus Valley Civilization were certain products. For example, indigo, which was really sought after in Mesopotamia and Egypt. Their handicrafts and jewelry were also very famous throughout the world.

They usually traded with Sumer and Egypt by trading with the Gulf State nations of Bahrain, Oman, and Dubai. They actually had a colony by the Persian Gulf. This civilization was so good at trading that we believe they even traded with China — which is pretty far away — but their ships were huge and they were very good navigators.

As the Bronze Age developed over time, bronze was in heavy demand. The main components of bronze are copper and tin. Tin is mainly found in the mountains of Afghanistan. This meant the Indus Valley Civilization started to expand over to Afghanistan in order to access the tin trade.

Tin was really sought after, but there's also another stone called lapis lazuli, which is really sought after by the Egyptians for its spiritual and aesthetic qualities.

Because of the trade with Afghanistan, land routes started to develop into Anatolia and Mesopotamia. As well, because of this trade in Afghanistan, new civilizations started to develop. They're not as advanced or as large as the three major ones, but they're still pretty advanced. For example, up here is what we call the Oxus Valley Civilization. The technical term used by archaeologists is the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex — you don't have to know this — it's called BMAC.

What makes this civilization important is that it interacts with the steppe people, called the Andronovo culture or related groups. So, as you can see, the Indus Valley Civilization, through its partners — the Oxus Valley Civilization and its colonies — now traded with the entire known world. There's no piece of the Western world that the Indus Valley Civilization does not touch.

Does that make sense? This is a world about 2500 to 2000 BCE. It's a world that's heavily interconnected. There are two major sources of demand. The Egyptians are extremely wealthy and they want these valuable jewelry and handicrafts for their burials — that's one source of heavy demand.

The main source of demand is actually Mesopotamia, where the city-states of Sumer are fighting wars against each other. In war, bronze makes a huge difference because bronze armor is much stronger than copper armor. So these two places are driving demand, and as such the Indus Valley Civilization is growing wealthy through trade.

Even though the Indus Valley Civilization is connected with Egypt and Mesopotamia, it's a radically different civilization.

The first thing that makes the Indus Valley Civilization interesting is that it's a huge area. It's actually, in terms of size, larger than Egypt and Mesopotamia combined. It has a population at its peak of about 5 million people. So this is a huge area.

What really is interesting is that we have found **no evidence of any organized warfare** in this region during this time. The reason is that this region is connected through trade.

We have found five major urban centers in the Indus Valley Civilization. These urban centers you can consider to be what we call value-adding processing centers. These are urban centers that specialize in taking raw goods — like metals and agricultural products — and turning them into finished products like jewelry and handicrafts to be shipped and traded, mainly with Mesopotamia, but also Egypt, and possibly China as well. It also goes north to the Oxus Valley Civilization, which then trades with the steppe people — the engine of culture, what we know as the Yamnaya people.

This trade network is very complex, and everyone is involved in some capacity.

That's the first thing that's interesting about the Indus Valley Civilization: they trade internally peacefully, and they trade externally peacefully as well. We have absolutely no evidence that they ever had organized warfare. We have actually no evidence that they had any intention of organized warfare — meaning we have not found any weapons of war. We have found tools and hunting weapons, but we have not found armor or helmets.

How do we know there's no organized warfare? We know there's a lot of organized warfare in Mesopotamia and other places because of what we've dug up. We look at cities and we see parts of the city that have been built on top of destroyed layers — meaning there might have been fires or warfare.

But with these five centers that we dug up, we have found them pretty intact. They were not built on top of or destroyed during the period when they were around. That's the first major piece of evidence.

The second major piece is we've dug up their graves. In other civilizations like the Yamnaya culture or Mesopotamia, they tend to bury warriors with their weapons — helmets, armor, etc. But when we dug up Indus Valley Civilization graves, first of all we don't find a distinct caste of warriors, and we don't find any helmets, armor, or weapons.

There's also evidence suggesting our evidence is not solid. They don't always bury their dead — some are buried, but not everyone. We don't know exactly where they buried them. We assume in the cities, but that may not be true.

So the evidence leans towards a peaceful civilization, but as we dig up more over time we may discover this was not true. Right now, the evidence suggests they were peaceful.

Also, what's really important about the Indus Valley Civilization is that this is a relatively new discovery. We've only known about them for the past 100 years. The Indians themselves never knew they existed. It was the British who discovered these cities and started the archaeological work to reconstruct their civilization.

The first thing that's interesting is that it was relatively peaceful compared to what happened in Mesopotamia.

The second thing that was interesting — and surprising — is that for a very large civilization, they were **egalitarian**. In Mesopotamia, when we look at the old cities like Ur and Uruk, there were these huge palaces and temples which suggested a concentration of power in the priesthood and in the king.

But when we look at these five urban centers of the Indus Valley Civilization, there are no palaces and no temples. In fact, these cities are extremely well-designed. There are walls around the city, and as you go into the city you're able to access every single part of the city. But if you go into a city in Sumer, you're only able to access the temple or the palace. Authority is centralized in Sumer, whereas authority is more decentralized in the Indus Valley.

If they were not engaged in warfare, why did they have walls — and many walls inside the cities? The answer is customs and taxes. This is again a value-adding processing center, and the way they survive economically is by collecting tolls and customs on people who trade. When you enter the city, you have to go to the customs house, pay what's required, and then you can access the city. That's how they centrally controlled the economy and trade in the Indus Valley.

These cities are amazing. These are the first people to invent the seated toilet — the private seated toilet — this is about the year 2800 BCE. If you go into these cities, you'll find that in private homes everyone has a private toilet, and the waste is put into a sewage system and the water flushes it out. That's pretty amazing.

These are people who are very concerned about the well-being of all its citizens. When we look at skeletal remains through DNA analysis, we've discovered that the majority of the people ate about the same high quality of food. There weren't these disparities between people as you would find in other civilizations.

At least half the people we think lived past the age of 55 — that was an amazing achievement back then.

In terms of sanitation, they have reservoirs. They also have public baths. They also had really amazing things called wind towers. I'm not sure if you've studied these wind towers, but they're basically air conditioners. What they do is these are high towers with a hole on top. The higher air is cooler, so this cool wind gets into the wind tower, comes down, and pushes out the hot air from the other side. This is what cools the houses in the summer. This is pretty advanced technology.

Another really cool thing about the Indus Valley Civilization is that even though it covers a huge area, they have standardized weights and measurements. Every brick they have is the same size, which means their buildings and structures are extremely stable and resilient. That's why 5,000 years later we still have their cities intact. You can actually go to the cities and the structures are still standing — that's how stable the technology was.

So this is a pretty advanced civilization.

Unfortunately, we don't know what their writing system is. If they had writing — and we can't know for sure — it was like Chinese or Indic: it is not fully phonological. The spoken language is not the same as the written language. In other words, it is impossible for us to decipher their written language. We don't have that many artifacts of writing. We have seals with characters written on them, but we don't have anything else.

The reason could be that if they wrote, it would be on palm leaves, which degrade very easily in the humidity — unlike papyrus in Egypt or cuneiform on clay tablets in Mesopotamia.

This is problematic because without access to the writing, we don't have access to the religion. We don't know what the religion is. And one thing that you've learned in this class that's very important is that religion or mythology is the operating system of the culture. It is the collective consciousness, the collective worldview that gives life to their culture and explains why they do what they do.

Unfortunately, we don't know their religion. So it's a complete mystery to archaeologists and scholars as to why this very advanced civilization was very peaceful and very egalitarian. We can only speculate.

I'm going to speculate as to why they were peaceful and egalitarian. Again, the key is the religion — what is the religion? Because that will give us clues as to their mentality and value system.

We know that the Mesopotamian people — their religion focused on struggle and achievement. We know that the Egyptians — their religion focused on the afterlife. This life that we live — what's important is not the here and now; what's important is what happens afterwards. That's why the Egyptians built pyramids and focused so much on funeral rites and burial goods.

So there had to be a religion in the Indus Valley that was different from the belief system in Sumer and Egypt — one that made it into a peaceful and egalitarian civilization.

My speculation is this: the key is to understand that this is a civilization based on trade. They've been traders ever since they were born as a culture — meaning they were trading for at least 7,000–8,000 years before they became a civilization.

If you're a trader, first of all you're able to access the entire world. We know for a fact they traded with Sumer and the Persian Gulf states. We can speculate they also traded with Egypt and with China based on their technology and trade networks.

As traders, they're able to see what's going on in Mesopotamia and Egypt. They know they built the pyramids in Egypt. They know there's massive warfare in Sumer. I would think that if you were a trader and you saw these things, you wouldn't be impressed. In fact, you might be disgusted — especially by the warfare in Mesopotamia.

This is the idea called the **dialectic**. I think this is a very important idea for us to appreciate how history is often driven by the dialectic — how Japan has responded to China, how Canada responds to the United States, how smaller English-speaking societies relate to a much larger neighbor. You often define yourself partly against what you see across the border.

I believe that's what happened with the Indus Valley Civilization. These traders saw what was happening in Egypt and Mesopotamia and they were kind of disgusted — especially by all this warfare in Mesopotamia. Warfare back then was pretty awful.

In Mesopotamia, the way they fight is with clay bullets from slingshots. Think of David and Goliath. If you're a trader, you'll see this warfare going on, people being beheaded, and they're doing it in the name of God. Every city-state has a patron god that it believes it must defend, honor, and protect.

If you're a trader and you see this, you don't think this is a good thing. You go to Egypt and you see these pyramids — extremely impressive — but you also see the consequences: massive inequality, massive corruption, and massive waste.

I think that by trading with these two civilizations and others, it reinforces the deep cultural values of the Indus Valley people and basically compels them towards peace and egalitarianism. That's what I believe happened.

There are other arguments — for example, maybe these people are just genetically peaceful and egalitarian. But I've made the argument in this class that from the onset of early human history we were peaceful, original people, but different geographic circumstances change our outlook. The steppe peoples — Yamnaya, Andronovo-related groups — lived in a harsh environment; over time that geography pushes cultures toward aggression, expansionism, and opportunism.

Now there are still inequalities in the Indus Valley Civilization. We know there are classes and wealth inequality, but relative to Mesopotamia and Egypt it was a relatively peaceful and egalitarian society.

As we discussed in previous classes, when you're a peaceful and egalitarian society — like Old Europe before the steppe invasions — you will eventually get invaded or pressured by other cultures.

For the longest time there was a theory — first proposed basically by the Nazis — called the Indo-Aryan Invasion. The idea is you have these white people sweep into India, conquer the people, and introduce civilization to the Indians. This is called the Indo-Aryan Invasion.

Even though this theory has been largely discredited in its crude form, there is a slight kernel of truth to this theory, which I will explain.

You have the Indus Valley Civilization, which is a very advanced civilization. The problem is their entire economy is especially based on trade.

We know about something called the **4.2 kiloyear event**, which is basically a massive climate change around the world. The 4.2 kiloyear event brought an end to Egypt’s Old Kingdom. It also brought an end to what we call the Akkadian Empire in Sumer. A man named Sargon of Akkad had conquered all of Sumer and built an empire, and because of this drought his empire collapsed.

Trade networks that the Indus Valley was heavily dependent on started to collapse. As a result, their cities — which are dependent on trade — start to depopulate. A lot of people start to return to the farms or migrate to the rest of India, to the Ganges.

Because of this climate change, the region itself is being affected heavily. There's drought; the rivers are drying up.

We learned in this class that over time all civilizations face eternal tensions. These can be divided into two major tensions.

The first is conflict among the classes — the idea of elite overproduction. You have too many people striving for limited status. The conflict is mainly between upper nobility and lower nobility. In the Indus Valley, we can imagine conflict between large trading houses and small trading houses, or internally among families.

The other social tension is the idea of "rat utopia" — especially conflict between the old and the young. The problem in the Indus Valley is you have too many old people living too long, which means fewer opportunities for achievement for the young. That creates conflict as well.

During this time there was massive social tension within the Indus Valley Civilization. Some people would even say it was a revolutionary time when old customs and norms were being overthrown.

Because of climate change, the people in the steppe culture are now forced to leave the steppes. They push into the Oxus Valley Civilization and over time they will assimilate it into themselves.

This is **not** an intentional military invasion. It's not that you have a general and an army that go in and conquer it. It's more opportunistic. At first you're trading, and as the Oxus Valley Civilization declines because of collapsing trade networks, they start to come in and push out the people.

The culture of these steppe people is very aggressive, so there are two strategies they would employ. The first is they might wipe out villages (which are already depopulated). The second is to come in, kill all the men, and marry all the women.

This is a gradual process of assimilation and cultural conquest, but it's not a military invasion.

After the steppe culture and the Oxus culture merge, this creates a new civilization and a new culture called **Proto-Indo-Iranian**.

They will go south to the Iranian plateau and eventually become the Persian people. They will invent a new religion that becomes a great world religion called Zoroastrianism. Zoroastrianism comes from this merger.

There are three distinct characteristics of the Proto-Indo-Iranian culture that become salient in the new Zoroastrian religion: the use of a ritual drink called Soma, the worship of fire (fire rituals), and horse sacrifice (warriors buried with their horses) — an emphasis on the warrior culture.

These people will also push into the Indus Valley Civilization. Over time — and this is a process that takes about 500 years — they'll push out the indigenous people. The indigenous people move into South India where they merge with the folk culture and the animism of the local people there.

What's amazing about this is that this process will create two major world religions that are still influential today.

The first we know as **Hinduism** — the merger (synchronization) of Proto-Indo-Iranian religion with the local Indus Valley religion.

As the Indus Valley people are pushed south, this will create a new religion called **Buddhism**.

Buddhism is just the major one; there are others being created as well through this process — Jainism and Sikhism. Basically this process created thousands of new religions.

This is what happened. It's not a military conquest or invasion, but it's a process of — you can argue — cultural genocide, where new people come in and over time, because they are aggressive, expansionist, and opportunistic, they will eventually take over the culture — mainly by marrying the local women (because the people who come in are basically males). They will marry the local women and either kill or enslave the local men. This is where we get the caste system from in India.

This is a more subtle explanation of the Indo-Aryan "invasion" theory. This is what scholars today largely accept happened.

What's really important is to avoid generalizations and simplifications because this is a process that took about 300–400 years, and there are different strategies employed by different people. Some of it was peaceful, some of it was violent. It's a mix. It's hard for us to generalize, but the overall general effect is the creation of Hinduism and Buddhism, which now are two of the major world religions.

The last thing I want to discuss is what is the legacy of the Indus Valley Civilization. They were a great civilization. Are they completely lost to us? What is their contribution?

The legacy of Egypt is the pyramids. The legacy of Mesopotamia is many things — they invented writing (cuneiform) and they introduced world literature like the Epic of Gilgamesh.

What I want to argue now is that the legacy of the Indus Valley Civilization is in their **spiritual practice** and **spiritual values**.

In classical Hinduism you get a picture of **maya** — the world of appearances as false or illusory — and **Brahman** as the true, absolute reality behind it. You get **karma**, **atman** (soul), **dharma**, **moksha** (liberation). Access to that system is mediated by Brahman priests, and the social hierarchy is often differentiated by skin color — partly because of the historical merger of lighter-skinned Proto-Indo-Iranians with darker-skinned Indus Valley people.

In this system, the kings (lower nobility) are unhappy because they must obey the Brahman priests. From this conflict you have the creation of Buddhism.

Remember, Siddhartha Gautama (the first Buddha) was actually a prince before he became the Buddha. The man most responsible for spreading Buddhism was Ashoka, the emperor of the Mauryan Empire, who institutionalized Buddhism as state religion and sponsored missionary projects.

These rulers don't like Hinduism because they see the Brahman class as a threat to their power. Buddhism follows a similar system except for one major difference: you don't need the Brahmans to access Nirvana. You can do it by yourself through spiritual guidance by monks.

Buddhism is in many respects a revolutionary religion to overthrow the power of the Brahman class.

In response to Buddhism, what did the Brahmans do? They created the caste system — these classes must separate from each other; they are not allowed to intermingle, not even allowed to touch each other or each other's food. These people at the very bottom are what we call today the Untouchables.

This system was developed by the Hindu priests (Brahmans) in response to the threat of Buddhism.

Over time, because Hinduism (like the Proto-Indo-Iranians) is aggressive, expansionist, and opportunistic, they will defeat Buddhism in this struggle — in part by adopting many of Buddhism's practices (yoga, meditation, Nirvana, etc.).

They will also start assimilating other religions. Most Indians believed in their folk local gods. Eventually all these gods will be assimilated into the Hindu hierarchy. That's why you have like a million gods in Hinduism — because they were all assimilated over time. The higher gods are the Hindu trinity / Brahman gods.

Across Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism you keep seeing the same kinds of themes — false or shifting reality, oneness, karma, dharma, reincarnation, impermanence. Those ideas are not strongly present in core Proto-Indo-European religion, but they are prominent in the Indian traditions. My speculation is that a lot of that common denominator traces back to something like a **Proto-Buddhist** religion in the Indus Valley — rooted in earlier animism and sharpened by the dialectic: reacting to the warfare, inequality, and corruption they saw in Mesopotamia and Egypt.

I would connect that instinct to something broader in human beings — a nostalgia for oneness and for a truer reality. Plato's Allegory of the Cave and the Christian idea of the Second Coming are different languages for a similar hunger.

Humans fundamentally seek spiritual understanding, driven by curiosity and imagination. Trade and globalization are inherent to human nature. That can lead to peace and egalitarianism, as in the Indus Valley, or to conflict — it depends on context.
